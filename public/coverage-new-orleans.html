New Orleans calls for service, with the delay made visible.

Louisiana911 maps citizen-initiated NOPD calls for service from the City of New Orleans open-data system. The source is published on a delayed daily schedule, so the map shows a dynamic age banner instead of presenting it as live dispatch data.

What Louisiana911 includes

The map uses the call's public item number, creation time, final and initial classifications, NOPD district information, published block address, and public geometry when available. Rows marked as officer-initiated are excluded from the public crime-and-safety view.

What gets filtered out

The official source includes routine administrative and officer activity that does not describe a useful public-safety event. Louisiana911 omits these generic final call types when their initial classification is also generic:

  • Area Check
  • Business Check
  • Complaint Other
  • Disturbance (Other)
  • Fugitive Attachment
  • Incident Requested by Another Agency
  • Medical
  • Mental Patient
  • Return for Additional Info
  • Tow Impounded Vehicle (Private)

If a generic final type began with a material initial classification—such as theft, battery, burglary, robbery, or a protection-order violation—the record stays visible under that initial classification. Filtering changes Louisiana911's display layer; it does not alter the official dataset.

Approximate and missing points

Data.NOLA sometimes supplies POINT(0 0) while still publishing an approximate block or intersection. Louisiana911 treats zero coordinates as missing and may geocode only that same public text. The original label remains unchanged, and the resulting map marker is dashed and explicitly approximate. A label that cannot be validated remains unmapped.

What a call classification means

A calls-for-service record reflects information available to dispatch at a point in time. It is not a finding of guilt, proof that a crime occurred, or a complete account of the responding agency's final disposition.
